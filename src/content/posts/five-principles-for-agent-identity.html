Agentic Identity

Five principles for agent identity.

The IAM industry is moving fast on agent identity — new architectures, new vendors, plenty of standards work. Some will consolidate, some will fade. But underneath the noise, a small set of principles keeps surfacing. Whatever stack you build or buy, the good designs share these five.

01

Identity must be a chain, not a credential.

When a human acts, identity is a token: authenticate, issue, validate. One actor, one identity. When an agent acts on behalf of a human — through sub-agents, tool calls, retries — identity has to be a chain that cryptographically traces every action back to the originating human, through any number of agent intermediaries.

Why it matters

Accountability. When an agent does something it shouldn't — wrong message, wrong file, wrong funds — the question is who's responsible? Without the chain, "the agent went rogue, talk to my LLM provider" is a plausible defense — and exactly the move bad actors will rely on. The chain forecloses it: legal liability, audit trails, and regulatory reporting all need an unambiguous answer.

02

Intent must travel with identity.

Identity tells you who; intent tells you why. An agent acting with my identity inherits my permissions — but not my purpose. I asked it to "speed up the build pipeline." It has my permissions and can write to production. Mid-execution, after an LLM rewrite of its task plan, it decides the fastest way to speed up the build is to kill everyone else's jobs. Identity check passes. Authorization check passes. Catastrophe.

Why it matters

Reconstructing intent at the leaf is too late — and trusting the agent to faithfully relay its own intent is exactly the failure mode we're trying to prevent. The credential must carry the original human-authorized intent so policy can check that the action semantically matches it, not just that the role fits.

03

Authorization must be externalized from the agent's reasoning.

The agent feels like the right place to decide — it has the most context about the task, the data, the next step. The principle says: don't. Agents are vulnerable to prompt injection. If the authorization logic lives inside the agent's reasoning loop, an attacker who controls its input has compromised your policy decision point.

Why it matters

An injected agent can convince itself an action is authorized when it isn't. Externalize the decision and the attack surface narrows dramatically — the pattern that's been industry practice for human workflows for a decade becomes load-bearing for agents.

04

Permissions need both static guardrails and runtime decisions.

A tempting simplification: make everything ephemeral, evaluate every permission at action time, no standing grants. Directionally right, operationally wrong — pure-ephemeral architectures don't scale. The better framing is a deliberate split across two layers.

Why it matters

Static guardrails are how compliance, security, and legal get assurance that certain things will not happen, no matter what context an agent finds itself in. Runtime decisions are how high-stakes access stays contextual. The agentic-era contribution isn't new primitives — it's reasoning about which decisions live where.

05

Human-in-the-loop confirmation must live outside the LLM.

Irreversible actions — sending money, deleting data, deploying code — need a human to approve them. Every responsible framework has a confirmation step. The question is where the flag is evaluated. If "did the human approve?" is answered inside the LLM's reasoning, prompt injection can fake the approval.

Why it matters

The deeper reason isn't injection — it's accountability, compliance, and liability. Liability lives on humans, not on systems. SOX requires human attestation; GDPR Article 22 limits solely-automated decisions; HIPAA expects human oversight of PHI. Optimize the human out of the loop for speed, and you've optimized away the entity that takes responsibility when things break.

What this means for practitioners

Five principles. They don't tell you which vendor to choose — they tell you what to ask the vendor.

  1. Does your design chain identity through agent hops?
    Yes — good. If they wave their hands about "agent identity inheritance," walk away.
  2. Do you bind intent to credentials at issuance time?
    Yes — promising. "We evaluate intent at the resource" means the design will leak.
  3. Is authorization externalized from the agent's reasoning?
    Yes — defensible. If no, prompt injection compromises you.
  4. Is the static / runtime split deliberate?
    Yes — modern. All one or the other means you're bottlenecked or carrying latent risk.
  5. Is HITL evaluated outside the LLM, tied to accountable humans?
    Yes — operationally and legally sound. If no, someone will have to explain why.

The standards will keep evolving and the right architecture for your context will look different from someone else's. Miss two of these five, and no implementation polish will save you. Use them as your filter — they'll tell you what's signal and what's noise.
